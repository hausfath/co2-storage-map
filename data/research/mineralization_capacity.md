# In-Situ Mineralization (ISM) Capacity: Narrative Companion

*Companion to `mineralization_capacity.json` (35 formations). Covers flood basalts, ophiolites/peridotites, rift volcanics, and offshore basalt.*

## 1. How ISM capacity estimates are constructed

Unlike sedimentary saline-aquifer or depleted-reservoir storage, where capacity is bounded by measured pore volume, injectivity, and a caprock seal, in-situ mineralization (ISM) capacity estimates in the literature are built from two structurally different methods that are frequently conflated:

**(a) Rock volume × Mg/Ca stoichiometry ("theoretical" method).** This is the dominant method for peridotite/ophiolite targets and most flood-basalt LIP-scale figures. The calculation is: estimate the volume of reactive mafic/ultramafic rock (area × thickness, sometimes to an arbitrary depth cutoff of a few km), multiply by rock density and the mass fraction of Mg- and Ca-bearing silicate minerals (olivine, pyroxene, Ca-plagioclase), and assume stoichiometric conversion of those cations to carbonate (magnesite, calcite, ankerite) via reactions such as Mg₂SiO₄ + 2CO₂ → 2MgCO₃ + SiO₂. This is the method behind Kelemen & Matter's (2008, *PNAS*) landmark Samail Ophiolite figure — ~50×10¹² t of peridotite in the Oman massif could in principle sequester ~30×10¹² t (30,000 Gt) CO₂ — and behind the wider Kelemen et al. (2011, 2019) syntheses that put the *global* peridotite/basalt theoretical ceiling at 10⁵–10⁸ Gt CO₂, several orders of magnitude above any credible deployable resource. These numbers answer "how much CO₂ *could* this rock hold if every reactive cation carbonated," not "how much CO₂ can actually be delivered to that rock and reacted within an engineering timeframe." They ignore injectivity (can fluid actually be pumped through the rock fast enough), access (surface infrastructure, depth, permitting), and the kinetic reality that most silicate carbonation reactions are slow unless enhanced (fracturing, elevated temperature/pCO₂, dissolved-CO₂ injection à la Carbfix).

**(b) Injectivity/porosity-limited method ("effective" or "site" method).** This is closer to the sedimentary-storage playbook and is used for the handful of formations with actual reservoir characterization: Columbia River Basalt (McGrail et al. 2006; PNNL 2024 stacked-reservoir study), Juan de Fuca Plate (Goldberg et al. 2008, 2010), and the Faroe Islands/Vøring basalt reservoir studies (Rosenqvist et al. 2023). Here the calculation uses measured or logged porosity in permeable zones (interflow rubble, brecciated/vesicular flow tops — porosities of 0.5–36% at the Vøring 642E borehole, up to ~20% in Juan de Fuca pillow basalts), a net reservoir thickness (commonly 20–100 m), pressure/injectivity limits, and CO₂ density at reservoir conditions, without assuming complete cation conversion. These figures land 2–4 orders of magnitude below the stoichiometric ceiling for the same formation and are the numbers that should anchor any atlas comparison across storage types (mineralization vs. sedimentary).

**Onshore vs. offshore cost divide.** The two methods also map onto a stark cost gradient. Onshore basalt/peridotite ISM with existing infrastructure — Iceland's Carbfix (Hellisheiði, operating since 2012, >95% mineralization within ~2 years), the Columbia River Basalt Wallula pilot (60% mineralized within ~2 years), and Kenya's Octavia Carbon/Cella project — cluster around an estimated **$10–30/tCO₂** for the mineralization/injection step under favorable conditions (co-located low-carbon power, shallow reactive basalt, existing wells). Offshore basalt storage (Juan de Fuca/Cascadia CarbonSAFE, generic mid-ocean-ridge basalt) is estimated at **$200–400/tCO₂**, reflecting deepwater drilling, marine monitoring, and platform/vessel costs — and that $200–400 figure is itself a best-case coastal-margin number (Cascadia Basin, ~2,600 m water depth but relatively close to shore); true open-ocean mid-ocean-ridge sites, thousands of km from any CO₂ source and in 2,000–4,000+ m water, would be considerably more expensive still and are essentially theoretical rather than deployable.

## 2. The tiering problem: theoretical vs. effective vs. practical

Sedimentary storage assessments (e.g., the USDOE/NATCARB-derived SRMS methodology used elsewhere in this atlas) have a well-established three-tier vocabulary: **theoretical** (total pore volume × CO₂ density, no engineering constraints), **effective** (theoretical reduced by geologic exclusion criteria — depth, seal, faulting), and **practical** (effective reduced by surface access, infrastructure, and regulatory constraints, often via a "storage efficiency factor" of a few percent applied to theoretical). Each tier is nested inside the one before it, and the ratios between tiers are reasonably well constrained by decades of saline-aquifer and EOR experience (commonly 1-10% of theoretical becomes practical).

ISM/mineralization has no equivalent, standardized three-tier system, and this is a genuine gap in the field rather than a labeling inconsistency this atlas can paper over:

- **Theoretical** in the mineralization literature (Kelemen-style rock-volume × stoichiometry) is not analogous to sedimentary "theoretical." Sedimentary theoretical capacity assumes CO₂ simply fills available pore space — a physically achievable (if impractical) state. Mineralization theoretical capacity assumes *complete conversion of every reactive cation in the rock volume to carbonate*, which for peridotite implies mineralizing rock down to several km depth over areas of tens of thousands of km² — a process that has never been observed to complete even locally and that would require millennia of natural weathering or an enormous, sustained engineered fluid-flow program. The 10⁵-10⁸ Gt global range is best understood as a physical upper bound on Earth's silicate-cation inventory, not a resource estimate in the sedimentary-storage sense.
- **Effective** capacity, where it exists at all for mineralization, is really the injectivity/porosity-limited method described above (McGrail, Goldberg, Rosenqvist et al.) — but this has only been computed for a handful of formations (Columbia River Basalt, Juan de Fuca, Faroe/Vøring basalt) out of the 35 in this inventory. For peridotite/ophiolite targets (Oman, New Caledonia, PNG, most others) no effective-tier number exists in the literature at all; every published figure for these is the stoichiometric theoretical ceiling.
- **Practical** capacity for mineralization is best evidenced empirically, from operating or piloted projects, rather than modeled top-down: Carbfix's Hellisheiði operation demonstrates ~95% mineralization of injected CO₂ within ~2 years at a specific, well-characterized site; the Wallula Basalt Pilot demonstrated ~60% mineralization within ~2 years for under 1,000 t injected; the Cascadia CarbonSAFE pre-feasibility study scoped a 50 Mt (0.05 Gt) 20-year *program*, five orders of magnitude below the Juan de Fuca theoretical ceiling of ~840-2,450 Gt. These bottom-up, project-scale numbers — not the stoichiometric ceilings — are the right anchor for any "how much CO₂ could mineralization plausibly deliver by mid-century" question.

The practical implication for this atlas: mineralization entries should be flagged distinctly from sedimentary SRMS entries whenever their `capacity_gt` field reflects a stoichiometric theoretical estimate (the great majority of formations here) rather than an injectivity-constrained effective estimate (Columbia River Basalt, Juan de Fuca/Cascadia, Iceland's dedicated CarbonSAFE-style figures) or a demonstrated practical throughput (Carbfix, Wallula, Kenya). Presenting a 30,000 Gt Samail Ophiolite theoretical number alongside a 148 Gt Columbia River Basalt effective number without this caveat would materially mislead a reader into thinking Oman is ~200x more "storage-ready" than Washington State, when in fact Oman's number is unconstrained by any engineering feasibility analysis at all.

## 3. Per-category summaries

### Flood basalts (12 formations)

Flood basalts (continental Large Igneous Provinces, LIPs) are the ISM category with the widest spread of assessment maturity. Only three of the twelve entries in this inventory have any published Gt figure at all: **Columbia River Basalt Group** (USA, 10-148 Gt CO₂, revised above), **Deccan/Rajmahal Traps** (India, 94-316 Gt combined per Vishal et al. 2021, though newer probabilistic work narrows this to ~41-76 Gt — see verification notes), and **Harrat Al-Shaam/Arabian basalt** (Saudi Arabia/Jordan, ~1-10 Gt, anchored on a Jizan-region estimate of ~4.2 Gt). The remaining nine — Siberian Traps (largest LIP on Earth by volume, ~2.5 million km² preserved extent), Paraná-Etendeka (South America/Africa), Karoo (southern Africa), Ethiopian-Yemen Traps/Afar, North Atlantic Igneous Province onshore component, Emeishan (China), and CAMP (eastern USA/Morocco) — have no dedicated mineralization capacity study at all despite enormous areal extent, and are flagged in the JSON as pure data gaps. The pattern that emerges is important: LIP *area* is a poor predictor of assessment status. Siberia's Traps, at 2-4 million km² original extent and up to 3.4 km of lava thickness, dwarf Columbia River Basalt (210,000 km²) by an order of magnitude, yet have zero dedicated CCS literature, reflecting remoteness/permafrost rather than geological unsuitability. Deccan illustrates the opposite lesson: large theoretical numbers (up to 316 Gt) collapse under scrutiny once dyke-network compartmentalization and shallow-depth constraints are applied — Princeton CMI's 2022 synthesis concludes "virtually none" of the Deccan's nominal rock volume is actually suitable for supercritical CO₂ injection.

### Ophiolites / peridotites (13 formations)

This category contains the single largest number in the entire inventory — the **Samail Ophiolite** (Oman/UAE), where Kelemen & Matter's (2008, *PNAS*) foundational calculation gives ~30,000 Gt CO₂ theoretical capacity from ~50×10¹² t of exposed peridotite, with wider syntheses (Kelemen et al. 2011/2019) placing global peridotite theoretical capacity at 10⁴-10⁵ Gt. This figure is verified against the primary literature (see verification_notes) and is correctly caveated in the JSON as a full-conversion stoichiometric ceiling, not an engineering estimate. Samail is also the only ophiolite in the inventory with active field deployment: **44.01**, the Omani startup and 2023 XPRIZE Carbon Removal winner, is injecting CO₂ into the Fizh massif at pilot scale, building on decades of academic characterization (Oman Drilling Project, ¹⁴C dating of natural carbonate veins showing average ages of ~26,000 years — i.e., demonstrably active natural carbonation on human timescales). Of the other twelve ophiolite entries — New Caledonia, Papua New Guinea, Zambales (Philippines), Troodos (Cyprus), Turkish (Kizildag/Anatolian), Mirdita/Balkan, California Coast Range, Bay of Islands (Newfoundland), British Columbia serpentinite belts, Thetford Mines (Quebec), Japan, and Kalimantan/Sulawesi — none has a disaggregated Gt figure. New Caledonia and PNG are both cited by Kelemen et al. as being among the world's largest peridotite bodies (comparable in scale to Oman) but lack site-specific quantification. British Columbia's Decar body stands out as the most operationally advanced non-Oman ophiolite target: its brucite-rich serpentinite (~13 wt% Mg(OH)₂) reacts with CO₂ at room temperature, and the Geoscience BC-funded CO2Lock program has run shallow pilot injection at the Atlin/Bobtail site — a genuinely different (shallow, near-surface) engineering approach from Oman's or Iceland's deep injection.

### Rift volcanics (5 formations)

This category is anchored by two of the world's most operationally mature ISM programs. **Iceland** (Carbfix, Hellisheiði) is the global reference case: Snæbjörnsdóttir & Gislason (2016) give a wide theoretical range of 60-7,000 Gt CO₂ (the upper bound being the full offshore EEZ mid-ocean-ridge-basalt volume, confirmed in this review as ~7,000 Gt "offshore Iceland within the Exclusive Economic Zone"), while the operationally demonstrated figure is what matters for the atlas: >95% of injected CO₂ mineralized within ~2 years at Hellisheiði since 2012, with the Coda Terminal (Straumsvík) import-and-store hub under development as a commercial-scale follow-on. **Kenya's Rift Valley** is the newest entrant and the first African mineralization injection: Octavia Carbon (DAC) and Cella Mineral Storage completed a first small-scale injection (~460 kg CO₂, initial test 2024-2025) near Lake Elementaita, paired with abundant local geothermal power (Olkaria, Menengai — the same reservoirs supplying ~50% of Kenya's grid). Reported regional capacity of ~400 Gt CO₂ (verified in this review, matching independent coverage citing "at least 400 gigatons" for the East African Rift) is a rift-wide theoretical figure, not a Kenya-specific engineered estimate. The remaining three rift/arc entries — Afar Depression (Ethiopia/Djibouti, geologically analogous to Kenya but unstudied), Hawaii (conceptually promising, young permeable shield basalt, but no dedicated study and active-volcano siting hazards), and Japan/Kurile arc basalts (lower-priority given more silicic, less Mg-rich arc chemistry) — are data gaps.

### Offshore basalt (8 formations)

Offshore basalt is simultaneously the category with the largest theoretical numbers and the weakest near-term economic case, because of the $200-400/tCO₂ cost premium discussed in Section 1. The **Juan de Fuca Plate** (offshore Pacific Northwest, USA/Canada) is the best-characterized site: Goldberg et al. (2008, 2010) estimate 134-668 Gt-C for the eastern and western ridge flanks combined, which converts (×3.67) to roughly 490-2,450 Gt CO₂; independent reporting of "at least 229 billion tons of carbon" (verified in this review) converts to ~840 Gt CO₂, matching the JSON's mid estimate closely. The Cascadia CarbonSAFE pre-feasibility study scoped a much smaller, genuinely practical-tier project (50 Mt CO₂ over 20 years) — a five-order-of-magnitude gap below the plate-wide theoretical ceiling that is the clearest illustration in this entire inventory of the theoretical/practical divide discussed in Section 2. The **Reykjanes Ridge** (offshore SW Iceland) hosts the first active sub-seabed basalt CO₂ storage pilot on a mid-ocean ridge segment (initial results reported 2024), extending Carbfix offshore. The **Vøring/Møre margins** (offshore Norway) and the onshore **Faroe Islands Basalt Group** (used as an analogue for the buried offshore sequence) are under active reservoir characterization by the University of Oslo's Njord/CO2Basalt project, with porosities of 0.5-36.2% measured in brecciated flow-top crusts at the 642E borehole — real petrophysical data, unlike most peridotite entries. Two entries are included as explicit theoretical-ceiling placeholders rather than site assessments: the **generic global mid-ocean-ridge system** (1,000-100,000 Gt, an extreme upper bound with "essentially zero near-term practical relevance" per the JSON's own suitability note) and the **Kerguelen Plateau** (Southern Indian Ocean, one of the largest oceanic-plateau LIPs on Earth, but remote, deep-water, and with no nearby CO₂ sources or any capacity study at all).

## 4. Verification notes

Six of the highest-leverage capacity figures in the inventory were spot-checked against primary and secondary literature via web search (July 2026):

1. **Samail Ophiolite, Oman (~30,000 Gt CO₂ theoretical)** — CONFIRMED. Kelemen & Matter (2008, *PNAS* 105(45):17295-300) state ~50×10¹² t peridotite in the Samail massif could mineralize ~30×10¹² t CO₂ if fully carbonated. Exact match to the JSON figure and to the primary source. No correction needed.
2. **Columbia River Basalt Group (10-100 Gt CO₂ as originally stated)** — PARTIALLY REVISED. McGrail et al. (2006) is associated with both a conservative 10-50 Gt figure and a separate, less-commonly-cited ~100 Gt figure; the 2024 PNNL/ScienceDirect stacked-reservoir study gives 36-148 Gt CO₂ specifically for the onshore Columbia River Basalt (with much larger, more speculative 500-2,500 Gt offshore-WA/BC and 1,000-5,500 Gt "Caribbean flood basalt" figures reported in the same study as separate provinces, not part of this entry). **JSON corrected**: high bound updated from 100 to 148 Gt CO₂ and capacity_basis text revised to cite the 2024 range explicitly.
3. **Deccan Traps + Rajmahal Traps (94-316 Gt CO₂ as originally stated)** — FLAGGED, not fully corrected. Vishal et al. (2021) 316 Gt combined figure confirmed. However, search turned up a more recent probabilistic reassessment (incorporating porosity/net-to-gross/volumetric uncertainty) narrowing the range further to ~41-76 Gt CO₂ (P10=43, P50=58, P90=74), below even the JSON's stated 94-150 Gt "practically accessible" reassessment. **JSON updated**: capacity_basis text now cites this narrower probabilistic range as a caveat, but the low/mid/high numeric fields were left at 94/200/316 pending a clear primary-source citation for the 41-76 Gt figure (the exact author/year of the probabilistic study could not be confirmed with full confidence from search snippets alone — likely a 2021-2025 extension of the Vishal group's "systematic capacity assessment" line of work, ScienceDirect S1750583621002103 or a related 2025 paper).
4. **Iceland / Carbfix (60-7,000 Gt CO₂ theoretical)** — CONFIRMED. Independent search confirms "up to 7000 Gt CO₂ ... offshore Iceland within the Exclusive Economic Zone" as the upper bound of the Snæbjörnsdóttir & Gislason (2016) range, consistent with the JSON. No correction needed.
5. **Juan de Fuca Plate (490-2,450 Gt CO₂, mid 840 Gt)** — CONFIRMED. Independent reporting of "at least 229 billion tons of carbon" for the plate converts (×3.67 C→CO₂) to ~840 Gt CO₂, matching the JSON's mid-point exactly. The Goldberg et al. 2008/2010 134-668 Gt-C range (→490-2,450 Gt CO₂) is internally consistent with this. No correction needed.
6. **Kenya Rift Valley (50-400 Gt CO₂)** — CONFIRMED (high bound). Independent search confirms reporting of "approximately 400 gigatons" (stated as a minimum) for East African Rift/Kenya basalt mineralization potential, matching the JSON's high bound. The low/mid bounds (50/200 Gt) could not be independently traced to a specific primary source beyond the JSON's own citation (Onyango et al. 2021) in the time available; flagged as lower-confidence but not contradicted by any source found.

No other formations' capacity figures were checked against primary literature beyond what is already cited in the JSON's `source` fields; the qualitative "no capacity study identified" data-gap entries (22 of 35 formations) were not independently re-verified, as their absence of a published figure is itself the finding.
